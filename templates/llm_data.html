Goal of the study

Identify the discrepancy between box-office performance and qualitative reception (critics/audiences) based on LLM-assigned comedy intensity scores.

LLM Data Collection

This study differs from the previous one only in how the Comedy variable is constructed. Because LLM-based annotation is computationally expensive, we limited the analysis to 500 films, selecting the most popular titles according to TMDB’s dynamic popularity score. This choice increases the likelihood that the model has prior knowledge of the films.


We used the Qwen2.5-7B model , a large language model (LLM), meaning an artificial intelligence system trained on vast amounts of text to analyze and generate language, chosen as a compromise between performance and computational feasibility.


Comedy scores, justifications, and self-reported knowledge levels were generated for all films. After removing uncertain or low-confidence cases through post-processing filters, the final dataset consists of N = 440 films.

Comedy Classification

For this analysis, we chose to classify comedy based on the prior knowledge of an LLM.


The LLM assigned each film a Comedy Score (1–10) based on the movie’s intent to provoke laughter, distinguishing pure comedy (10) from genre hybrids (8–9), balanced dramedy/action (5–7), and mostly serious films with only comic relief (1–4). To avoid overestimation and guessing, it returned 0 when it lacked sufficient knowledge, along with a self-reported knowledge level and a brief justification.

Results

The regression shows that the level of comedy in a film increases slightly with its log box-office revenue (b = 0.004, R² = 0.000), but the result is not significant (p = 0.698). The level of comedy also decreases with its press rating (b = -0.378, R² = 0.002), although this effect is not significant (p = 0.359). In contrast, the level of comedy decreases significantly with audience rating (b = -0.560, p < 0.05, R² = 0.009).
