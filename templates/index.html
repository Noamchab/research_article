About this project

This research project investigates whether comedy is systematically devalued in cinema and where that devaluation occurs.


Using large-scale data (box office, audience and critic ratings), genre measures, and LLM-based classifications, the study shows that comedy tends to generate divergent forms of interest among critics and the general public, and that it is not chiefly penalized in award outcomes but filtered out upstream, through institutional selection at Cannes and the César Awards.

Why Comedy?

Since Antiquity, theater has been divided into two main genres: comedy and tragedy. Philosophers and critics traditionally considered tragedy the "noble" genre, while comedy was viewed as lighter, sometimes inferior entertainment.


This project explores this cultural hierarchy not through ancient theater, but through modern cinema, analyzing whether comedy is still underestimated compared to tragedy.

Here are the classical definitions of comedy and tragedy:

Comedy

Comedy in the Neoclassical period typically featured ordinary or middle-class characters. It aimed to entertain and to correct manners through laughter. The plot often relied on misunderstandings, social satire, and ridicule of human weaknesses, with a happy ending.

Tragedy

Tragedy was considered the highest genre, focusing on noble characters. It depicted inescapable conflicts, destructive passions, and fate leading to inevitable downfall. The aim was to inspire awe and provoke catharsis in the audience.

Problematic

To what extent is comedy symbolically devalued in contemporary cinema, and through which mechanisms does this devaluation operate, including popular reception, critical evaluation, and institutional recognition?
